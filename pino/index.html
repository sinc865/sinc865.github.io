Abstract

Generating realistic group interactions involving multiple characters remains challenging as group size expands, because interaction complexity grows combinatorially. Existing conditional diffusion models tackle this by incrementally generating motions conditioned on previously generated characters, but they rely on a single shared prompt; this limits nuanced control and often yields oversimplified interactions.

We introduce PINO, a training-free framework that decomposes complex group behavior into semantically relevant pairwise interactions and leverages pretrained two-person diffusion models. During noise optimization, physics-based penalties enforce plausibility—preventing overlap or penetration—while allowing precise user control over orientation, speed, and spatial relationships.

Comprehensive evaluations show that PINO produces visually realistic, physically coherent, and highly adaptable multi-person interactions, making it well-suited for animation, gaming, and robotics.

Approach

PINO addresses multi-character interaction by decomposing it into pairwise motion-generation steps:

  1. Start from a pretrained two-person diffusion model without further training.
  2. To add a new character, form a pair with an existing one, supply a text prompt describing only that pair, and generate their motion.
  3. Optimize the initial noise so the new motion respects group-level constraints (distance, orientation, no overlap).
  4. Repeat iteratively to construct interactions of arbitrary size, or to extend sequences.

Physics and spatial penalties are enforced during noise optimization, yielding fine-grained control while keeping the diffusion model unchanged.
